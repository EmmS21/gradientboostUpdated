Probability





We generally think of probability as a real value between 0 and 1. The value can also be expressed as a percentage between 0 and 100%. This value represents the notion that some things are more likely to occur than others and gives us the language to quantify the uncertainty of events.




Let’s try and look at this from a more practical perspective; Let’s assume we have a six sided die. Each time we roll the die we call this a trial or an event. We know that there are 6 possible outcomes. The die could land on any number between 1 and 6. The likelihood of the die landing on any given number/outcome is the probability




Assuming this is a fair dial, to get the probability of the die landing in a given number. Let’s assume the number is 4, we divide the number of times 4 appears in the die by the total number of possible outcomes. In this case we would divide 1 by 6 giving us a probability of 0.166 of the die landing on the number 4.




Put in a more concise way, the probability of an outcome is the proportion of times the outcome would occur if we observed the random process an infinite number of times.




Frequentism versus Bayesianism





The frequentist defines probability in terms of frequencies of events. If there is no set of identical trials, there is no probability. If need be, the frequentist will create a fictitious/hypothetical population so that she can meaningfully talk about frequencies.


Frequentism is philosophically safe, but frustrating because it limits the scope of probability to physical systems that are either random (like atomic decay) or so unpredictable that we model them as random (like a tumbling die). Anything involving people is pretty much off the table.




The alternative to frequentism is Bayesianism. Through this philosophy probability is defined as a degree of belief that an event will occur. This is a rather intuitive way to think about probability. In bayesianism we have prior beliefs, which are certain facts in our hypothesis space that are more plausible than others. These beliefs however change as we observe more instances that either prove or disprove our prior beliefs. Our updated/final beliefs are referred to as our posterior beliefs.




By this definition, the notion of probability can be applied in almost any circumstance. One difficulty with Bayesian probability is that it depends on a person’s state of knowledge; people with different information might have different degrees of belief about the same event. For this reason, many people think that Bayesian probabilities are more subjective than frequency probabilities.

Rules of Probability





For frequency probabilities, we can derive rules that relate probabilities of different events. Probably the best known of these rules is P(A and B) = P(A) P(B), where P(A and B) is the probability that events A and B both occur. This formula is easy to remember; the only problem is that it is not always true.




This formula only applies if A and B are independent, which means that if I know A occurred, that doesn’t change the probability of B, and vice versa.




For example, if A is tossing a coin and getting heads, and B is rolling a die and getting 1, A and B are independent, because the coin toss doesn’t tell me anything about the die roll. But if I roll two dice, and A is getting at least one six, and B is getting two sixes, A and B are not independent, because if I know that A occurred, the probability of B is higher, and if I know B occurred, the probability of A is 1.




When A and B are not independent, it is often useful to compute the conditional probability, P(A|B), which is the probability of A given that we know B occurred:




P(A|B) = P(A and B)/P(B)




From that we can derive the general relation:




P(A and B) = P(A) P(B|A)




This might not be as easy to remember, but if you translate it into English it should make sense:
“The chance of both things happening is the chance that the first one happens, and then the second one given the first.”




There is nothing special about the order of events, so we could also write P(A and B) = P(B) P(A|B) These relationships hold whether A and B are independent or not. If they are independent, then P(A|B) = P(A), which gets us back where we started. Because all probabilities are in the range 0 to 1, it is easy to show that P(A and B) ≤ P(A)




To picture this, imagine a club that only admits people who satisfy some requirement, A. Now suppose they add a new requirement for membership, B. It seems obvious that the club will get smaller, or stay the same if it happens that all the members satisfy B
